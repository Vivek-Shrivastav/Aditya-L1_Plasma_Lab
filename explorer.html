Mission Context

Why the L1 Lagrange Point?

The Sun–Earth L1 point, located approximately 1.5 million km sunward of Earth (roughly 1% of the Earth–Sun distance), is a dynamically favoured location for solar monitoring. A spacecraft at L1 can maintain a near-constant view of the Sun and sample the solar wind before it reaches Earth's magnetosphere.

🌐
Gravitational Balance
At L1, the gravitational pull of the Sun and Earth plus centrifugal force are in equilibrium, allowing a spacecraft to remain there with minimal station-keeping fuel expenditure.
☀️
Continuous Solar View
No Earth or lunar occultation at L1 enables continuous, uninterrupted observation of the Sun and sampling of the unperturbed solar wind — ideal for space weather monitoring.
💨
~20–60 Minute Warning
Solar wind structures detected at L1 typically reach Earth's magnetosphere 20–60 minutes later, providing an advance warning window for geomagnetic storm prediction.
🔭
Remote vs. In-Situ
Remote-sensing instruments (VELC, SUIT, HEL1OS, SoLEXS) observe the Sun from a distance. In-situ instruments (ASPEX, PAPA, MAG) directly sample the plasma and fields at L1 — the spacecraft is inside the measurement medium.
🧲
Interplanetary Magnetic Field
The IMF at L1 reflects the solar magnetic field carried outward by the solar wind. Its orientation (particularly the Bz component) governs geomagnetic coupling efficiency.
⚛️
Energetic Particle Access
L1 sits outside Earth's magnetosphere, providing access to the full energy spectrum of solar energetic particles (SEPs) and galactic cosmic rays without geomagnetic shielding effects.

Platform

Spacecraft Configuration

Aditya-L1 carries seven payloads. Remote-sensing instruments are permanently Sun-facing; in-situ instruments sample the local plasma environment at L1.

Instrument Placement Rationale

Remote-sensing instruments point continuously toward the Sun, observing solar atmospheric phenomena at photospheric, chromospheric, and coronal levels.

In-situ instruments are oriented to sample the local plasma and electromagnetic environment at L1. Unlike telescopes, they measure what flows through and around the spacecraft.

The Magnetometer is deployed on a dedicated boom to minimise magnetic interference from the spacecraft bus and electronic subsystems.

  • ASPEX — Solar wind ions, suprathermal & energetic particles
  • PAPA — Solar wind electrons & ions (moments)
  • MAG — Vector interplanetary magnetic field (boom-mounted)
  • VELC / SUIT / HEL1OS — Remote-sensing, Sun-facing

Archive & Access

Data Release Model

Aditya-L1 data is archived and distributed through ISRO's Space Science Data Centre (ISSDC) via the PRADAN (PRocess Archive and Data ANalysis) portal. The release model is designed for broad scientific access.

📡 Archive Portal

Data is hosted on the ISSDC PRADAN portal. Registered users can browse, query, and download datasets. PRADAN URL: pradan.issdc.gov.in (verify current URL via ISRO official channels).

⏱ Target-of-Opportunity

For significant solar events (major flares, CMEs, SEP events), selected data may be released on an expedited ~24–48 hour timeline following event detection and preliminary processing.

🔓 Proprietary Period

Aditya-L1 operates with a relatively short proprietary period compared to some international missions. Processed Level-2 data is released for community access on a rolling basis after calibration validation by the PI teams.

📦 Data Levels

Level-0: Raw telemetry. Level-1: Calibrated instrument counts / physical units. Level-2: Science-ready data products (moments, spectra, magnetic field vectors). Always use the highest available level for analysis.

📋 Milestone Releases

ISRO/ISSDC periodically announces milestone dataset releases corresponding to specific observation periods or event catalogues. Monitor official ISRO and ISSDC communications for such announcements.

⚠ Session Timeout

PRADAN sessions may timeout after approximately 30 minutes of inactivity. Plan bulk downloads accordingly. Use the bulk download mechanism for large datasets to avoid repeated re-authentication.

Methods

Analysis Workflow

A recommended step-by-step pipeline for working with Aditya-L1 in-situ plasma data, from initial data discovery through to scientific interpretation.

01

Discover Datasets

Browse the ISSDC PRADAN portal. Filter by instrument (ASPEX/PAPA/MAG), date range, and data level. Cross-reference ISRO science team announcements for validated dataset versions.

PRADAN ISSDC
02

Download Data (Mind Session Timeout)

PRADAN sessions timeout after ~30 minutes. For large data pulls, use the bulk/batch download facility. Verify file checksums after download. Keep notes on which data version (Level-1 vs Level-2) you downloaded.

CDF files FITS files ~30 min timeout
03

Understand Data Levels

Level-1: Calibrated physical quantities in instrument coordinates. Units and calibration applied, but no higher-level processing. Level-2: Science-ready derived quantities (plasma moments, field vectors in standard frames). Prefer Level-2 for most analyses.

L1: Calibrated L2: Science-ready
04

Set Up Analysis Environment

Recommended stack for in-situ solar wind data:

Python 3.x NumPy Matplotlib SunPy SpacePy (CDF) Astropy (FITS) SpiceyPy (SPICE)

Install CDF library and spacepy.pycdf for CDF file reading. Use spiceypy with ISRO/SPICE kernels for coordinate transforms.

05

Core Analysis: Time Series

Plot plasma moments: proton density (np), bulk speed (Vsw), proton temperature (Tp), and IMF components vs. time. Use matplotlib.dates and ensure UTC time handling. Identify structures: stream interfaces, shocks, ICMEs.

n, V, T time series IMF Bx By Bz Event identification
06

Flux & Spectral Analysis

Produce differential energy flux spectra (counts or physical flux vs. energy channel) from SWIS and STEPS data. Energy–time spectrograms (colour-coded flux vs. time and energy) reveal particle acceleration events and SEP onset characteristics.

Differential flux E-t spectrogram Spectral index fits
07

Directional & Pitch-Angle Analysis

Combine STEPS 6-directional fluxes with MAG B-vector to construct pitch-angle distributions (PADs). Anisotropy index and PAD symmetry indicate particle injection geometry and magnetic connectivity quality.

PAD construction B-field alignment Anisotropy index
08

Multi-Instrument Correlation

Correlate in-situ plasma events with remote-sensing data (VELC/SUIT) for source region identification. Cross-reference SEP events with NOAA/GOES X-ray flare catalogs, LASCO CME catalogs (CACTus/CDAW), and HEK solar event databases for context.

VELC/SUIT context LASCO CME catalog NOAA flare list HEK database

Open Problems

Key Scientific Questions

Aditya-L1's in-situ plasma dataset uniquely addresses the following outstanding questions in solar and heliospheric physics.

01
How does the solar wind ion composition (especially α/p ratio) evolve between the corona and 1 AU?
Probe: SWIS α/p ratio vs. solar wind speed and source region type
02
What acceleration mechanisms operate at interplanetary shocks and within CIR/CME structures to produce suprathermal populations?
Probe: STEPS spectral indices and timing relative to shock passages
03
What is the origin and spatial extent of the suprathermal seed population for SEP events?
Probe: STEPS pre-event background + SWIS suprathermal tails
04
How does magnetic connectivity to SEP source regions vary with solar wind structure and Heliospheric Current Sheet proximity?
Probe: MAG sector polarity + STEPS onset anisotropy + Parker spiral modeling
05
What is the relationship between IMF Bz orientation, solar wind dynamic pressure, and geomagnetic storm intensity?
Probe: MAG Bz + SWIS V/n combined with ground Dst index
06
How are electrons and ions heated differently in the solar wind, and does this vary across different wind types?
Probe: PAPA electron Te vs. SWIS proton Tp in fast/slow wind
07
What is the detailed 3D magnetic structure of ICMEs at 1 AU, and do they preserve their coronal flux rope topology?
Probe: MAG flux rope fitting + PAPA bi-directional electron strahls
08
How do Alfvénic fluctuations and turbulence in the solar wind affect particle transport and scatter mean free paths?
Probe: MAG power spectral density + STEPS directional anisotropy decay

Data Caveats

Archive Access & Analysis Cautions

Working with space mission data requires careful attention to instrument characteristics, data formats, and archive behaviour. Do not assume values not explicitly documented.

⏰
Session Timeout (~30 minutes) PRADAN portal sessions expire after approximately 30 minutes of inactivity. Re-authenticate before initiating large downloads. Use bulk download tools where provided.
📂
Do NOT Assume File Formats CDF and FITS are common formats for space physics data, but verify the actual file format for each instrument and data level in the official ISSDC documentation before writing read routines.
🕐
Do NOT Assume Data Cadence Instrument sampling cadences may vary by mode, operational configuration, or event-triggered burst modes. Never hard-code time step assumptions. Parse timestamps from file headers.
🌐
Coordinate System Clarity Confirm whether magnetic field and velocity data are in RTN, GSE, or GSM coordinates. Comparisons with other datasets (ACE, Wind, DSCOVR) require consistent coordinate transforms. Use SPICE kernels for accurate transforms.
⚡
Spacecraft Charging Effects Low-energy (especially electron) measurements below a few tens of eV can be affected by spacecraft potential. Review payload calibration notes for contamination thresholds before interpreting sub-100 eV features.
📄
Always Refer to Official Payload Documentation This site is a reference guide only. For instrument-specific geometric factors, energy table definitions, noise floors, and calibration procedures, always consult the official payload/instrument documentation distributed by the PI teams via ISRO/ISSDC.
🔢
Unknown / Undocumented Values Are Labelled Where specific values (exact cadence, sensitivity thresholds, angular resolution) are not publicly documented or confirmed, they are deliberately omitted or labelled "refer to documentation" in this guide. Do not fill gaps with assumptions.
