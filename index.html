🛰️ What is Aditya-L1?

India's first dedicated solar observatory, launched by ISRO on 2 September 2023. It carries seven scientific instruments — including SWIS (Solar Wind Ion Spectrometer) and MAG (Magnetometer) — to study the Sun continuously from a stable halo orbit around the L1 Lagrange point.

💨 What is Solar Wind?

A continuous stream of charged particles (plasma — mostly protons and electrons) flowing outward from the Sun's corona at 300–800 km/s. It carries the Interplanetary Magnetic Field (IMF) and drives space weather events that can affect satellites, power grids, and GPS.

📍 Why L1?

The Sun–Earth L1 point (1.5 million km sunward) is where gravitational forces balance, allowing a spacecraft to orbit while keeping the Sun in constant view. A satellite here gives ~30–60 minutes advance warning of incoming space weather before it hits Earth.

📖 Space Weather Primer

                        

                            

                                
What is Plasma? ▾

                                

                                    

                                        The fourth state of matter — a gas of ions and free electrons that respond collectively to electromagnetic fields. The solar wind is a tenuous plasma (~5 protons/cm³) streaming at 400–600 km/s.
                                    

                                

                            

                            

                                
Solar Wind Speed Classes ▾

                                

                                    

                                        Slow wind (~300–500 km/s): originates from coronal streamer belts.

                                        Fast wind (~600–800 km/s): from coronal holes (open field regions).

                                        CME-driven: can exceed 1000 km/s; strongest storm driver.
                                    

                                

                            

                            

                                
Geomagnetic Storms ▾

                                

                                    

                                        Caused by sustained southward IMF Bz reconnecting with Earth's magnetosphere. Classified G1–G5 (NOAA). Risks: HF radio blackouts, satellite drag, induced currents in power grids, aurora.
                                    

                                

                            

                        

                    

                


                

                    
🔬 Glossary

                    

                        
Bz

                        
North–south component of the IMF. Critical storm predictor. Southward (negative) Bz drives geomagnetic activity.


                        
CME

                        
Coronal Mass Ejection — a massive burst of magnetised plasma from the Sun; the most powerful space weather driver.


                        
CIR

                        
Co-rotating Interaction Region — where fast and slow solar wind streams collide and compress, forming high-density structures.


                        
IMF

                        
Interplanetary Magnetic Field — the Sun's magnetic field carried into the heliosphere by the solar wind.


                        
nT

                        
Nanotesla — unit of magnetic field strength. 1 nT = 10⁻⁹ T. Typical IMF: 5–10 nT; strong storm fields: 20–50 nT.


                        
Plasma β

                        
Ratio of thermal to magnetic pressure. β > 1 means plasma pressure dominates; β < 1 means magnetic pressure dominates.


                        
Alfvén Speed

                        
Speed at which perturbations travel along magnetic field lines: VA = B / √(μ₀ρ). Determines wave propagation in the plasma.
